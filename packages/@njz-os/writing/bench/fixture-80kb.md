## Section 1 — The opening

The lamp on the desk was the only honest light in the room. Everything
else had been arranged to flatter — the corner where the bookcase met
the wall, the soft chair angled just so. The lamp's bulb was bare,
because Eleanor wanted to see the shape of things, not their dressing.

She poured two fingers of whisky into a glass that was not for whisky.
The glass was, by intent, for a cocktail she had never made and would
never make again. She drank the whisky anyway, because the glass was
clean and because the bottle was within arm's reach.

> "There are sentences in this manuscript I would not write again," she
> told the dog. The dog stared at her, unmoved.

She had been writing for forty-six minutes. The chapter would be
finished tonight or it would never be finished. She had decided this on
the way home, walking past the lit windows of the bakery, and she had
decided it the way people decide things at thirty-four — quickly and
with the faintest tremor of fear.

## Section 2 — Lists and notes

Things Eleanor knew about the chapter:

1. It ended in a barn.
2. Two characters had to be alone in that barn.
3. One of them had to want to leave.
4. Neither of them could be allowed to leave first.
5. The smell of the barn — hay, leather, the rust of a forgotten plough — had to be on the page by the third paragraph.
6. The chapter was for her sister, who had not asked for it.

Things Eleanor did not know:

- whether her sister would read it
- whether the barn was a real barn or one she had stitched together from photographs and the smell of a stable she once visited in Brittany
- whether the dog, who watched her now with the soft persistence of dogs, was capable of judging her writing

She typed the first sentence, looked at it, and deleted it. She typed
it again, more slowly. She looked at it. She left it alone.

## Section 3 — A dialogue

"You came back," Marta said.

"You knew I would," Joran answered. The barn smelled of hay and leather
and the rust of a forgotten plough.

"I knew you might."

"That's not the same as knowing I would."

She watched him for a long time, the way you might watch a child
deciding to climb a wall too tall for him. Marta had loved him with
the kind of love that becomes architecture: she had built rooms in
herself for him, made a stair where there was none. He had walked
through these rooms like a guest who did not know the door he had come
in by.

"I'm leaving in the morning," she said.

"I know."

"You should know I'm not asking you to stop me."

"I wasn't going to."

That, she thought, was the first true sentence between them in a year.

## Section 4 — A long descriptive passage

The barn was older than the house and older than the orchard and older
than the road that brought them both here. It had been built by a
grandfather whose name nobody now bothered to keep — only the date
1879 was carved, in a small, careful hand, into the upright beam by the
door. The wood smelled of decades of weather and the slow patient labour
of horses. The smell was strongest near the floor, where the hay had
become, over time, a thick yellow rot that the new hay simply lay on
top of, generation after generation, the way the years of a forest lie
on top of each other in moss.

In one corner there was an old plough, kept there because nobody had
quite decided to throw it away and nobody had quite decided to use it.
Its iron parts had rusted to a dark red that was almost the colour of
old blood. The wooden handles were smooth from a thousand grips and
worn down at one end where someone — a left-handed grandfather, perhaps
— had pulled and pulled. Beside the plough stood two empty oil cans, a
length of stiff leather harness, and an old wheelbarrow that had not
held anything but dust in twenty years.

The two of them stood in the middle of all this — the leather, the
hay, the rust, the dust — and the barn breathed around them as old
buildings breathe, with a slow internal creak that was almost
companionable.

## Section 5 — Interior monologue

Marta thought: there are things you say at twenty-two that you can
spend the next decade quietly unsaying. Some of them in letters never
sent. Some of them in the long pauses on the telephone when you tell
your mother you are happy. Some of them in the choice of a third
glass of wine, or a fourth.

She thought: he came back. That was what she had asked for at
twenty-two and refused for herself at twenty-eight and prayed for at
thirty-one. He came back. Now the asking was old and worn and the
prayer was a shape inside her ribs that she had taught herself to
carry the way you teach yourself to carry a limp.

She thought: I will write him a letter in the morning. Then she
remembered there would not be a morning here, only a different room
with a different lamp.

## Section 6 — Closing

Eleanor read what she had written. She drank what was left in the
glass that was not for whisky. The dog had stopped watching her. The
lamp gave its honest light over the page.

She thought: it is finished, and it is not finished, and tomorrow I
will know which one was true.

She closed the laptop. The room around her was suddenly larger and
darker, and for a moment she felt the way she had felt as a child the
first time she stayed up later than the rest of the house — that the
night was a country with its own laws.

She left the lamp on for the dog.
